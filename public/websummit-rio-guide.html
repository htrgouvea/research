Transportation

GETTING AROUND EFFICIENTLY

Rio has several ways to get around. Here's what first-timers need to move efficiently during event week — and what to avoid.

🚗

Uber & 99

The primary way to get around. Download both apps — 99 is sometimes cheaper and has more cars in Barra. Card payment works perfectly with both.

Recommended
🚇

Metro

Great for Zona Sul (Ipanema, Copacabana, Centro). Does not reach Barra da Tijuca. Use for day-to-day movement between beaches and downtown meetings.

Zona Sul only
✈️

From the Airport (GIG)

Galeão Airport is on Ilha do Governador. Uber to Ipanema: ~50 min, ~R$60–80. To Barra da Tijuca: ~70 min, ~R$80–120 depending on traffic.

Plan ahead
🚡

VLT (Downtown Tram)

Modern streetcar connecting the Centro area. Useful for meetings in Porto Maravilha or Lapa. Easy QR code payment at stations.

Downtown

⏰

Always Add a 30-Minute Buffer

Av. das Américas (Barra) can be brutal from 7–10am and 5–8pm. If coming from Zona Sul, budget 60–90 min total. Never schedule back-to-back meetings across different parts of the city without a buffer. Leave early or wait out the rush.

Essential Tips

SAFETY & STREET SMARTS

Rio requires situational awareness — not paranoia. These simple habits will keep you stress-free throughout the week.

📵

Keep your phone out of sight on the street

Don't walk around staring at your phone in busy areas. Use it inside buildings, in Ubers, or at restaurants. In Copacabana, Lapa, and Centro, this is especially important. Look up and be aware.

💍

Skip the flashy jewelry and watches

Leave expensive watches, rings, and visible jewelry at home or in the hotel safe. Keep a digital copy of your passport on your phone and leave the physical one at the hotel.

🚕

Always use Uber or 99 at night

Wait inside until your car arrives. Verify the license plate before getting in. Ride apps are far safer than flagging taxis on the street at night in any neighborhood.

💳

Card payments everywhere — carry minimal cash

Brazil is highly digitized. International credit cards work at 95%+ of places. Withdraw small amounts only from ATMs inside banks or shopping malls. Avoid street ATMs at night.

☀️

Sunscreen and hydration are non-negotiable

June in Rio is warm but milder than summer (22–28°C / 72–82°F). Some days may be rainy — it's the start of the dry season. Inside Riocentro it's ice-cold from AC, so layer up. Bring a light jacket and SPF 50+. Drink water constantly.

🌊

Beach smarts

Bring only the essentials: towel, sunscreen, a little cash. Don't bring your laptop, camera, or expensive watch. Stick to Zona Sul beaches (Ipanema, Leblon, Arpoador) during daylight hours.

Rio's universities are not just academic institutions — they are active engines of entrepreneurship, deep tech, and some of the most important research in Latin America. Several of Brazil's most iconic tech companies trace their origins here.

🎓

UFRJ — Federal University of Rio de Janeiro

One of Brazil's top universities and a powerhouse of engineering, computer science, and applied research. Its alumni network is deeply embedded in Rio's tech ecosystem.

Notable spin-off: VTEX — the global commerce platform (NYSE: VTEX) was founded by UFRJ alumni and is now used by brands like Whirlpool, Walmart, and AB InBev. A living proof that world-class companies can emerge from Brazilian academia.

Founded 1920 · Engineering & CS
✝️

PUC-Rio — Pontifical Catholic University

One of the most prestigious private universities in Latin America, consistently ranked among Brazil's top research institutions. Known for strong programs in computer science, engineering, design, and social sciences.

PUC-Rio is home to Tecgraf, one of Brazil's oldest and most productive technology research labs, with deep ties to Petrobras and industrial simulation. A major source of technical talent for Rio's startup ecosystem.

Research · Tecgraf Lab

IMPA — Institute for Pure and Applied Mathematics

A world-renowned research institute and UNESCO category-2 center. Home to Fields Medal winner Artur Avila — the only Brazilian to have won math's highest honor. IMPA is a global reference in pure mathematics and is increasingly bridging into applied domains through IMPA Tech, its technology and data science arm focused on AI, machine learning, and quantum computing applications.

If you're building in deep tech or AI, IMPA is a serious conversation partner.

Deep Tech · AI · IMPA Tech

Payments

WHAT IS PIX — AND HOW TO USE IT

Pix is Brazil's instant payment system, created by the Central Bank in 2020. With 160M+ users, it has replaced cash for virtually everything — street food, taxis, restaurants, rent. Understanding Pix is essential for navigating Rio.

⚡

What is Pix?

Think of it as Brazil's version of Zelle or Venmo — but instant, free, and accepted absolutely everywhere, 24/7, including weekends and holidays. Payments settle in seconds. A QR code is all you need to pay or receive.

Free · Instant · Universal
🌍

The Foreigner Problem

Pix normally requires a Brazilian bank account and a CPF (local tax ID). Most foreigners don't have either. But there are workarounds — and they're getting better every year.

Has solutions ↓
💙

Wise — Best Option for Most

Open a Wise account before you travel. Wise now supports full Pix features: pay via QR code, send to any Pix key, scan and pay directly from the app. Fund it with your home currency and Wise converts to BRL at the mid-market rate. Setup takes under 5 minutes.

Recommended
🔵

Revolut

If you're already a Revolut user, you can hold BRL in your account and use it for card payments across Brazil. Revolut supports BRL transfers, though direct Pix QR code scanning may be limited depending on your account type. Good backup option.

Good backup
📱

WanderWallet & PicPay

WanderWallet is built specifically for tourists — it lets you use Pix without a CPF, including Pix-Saque (cash withdrawals via Pix at ATMs and lottery shops). PicPay also allows foreigners to register with passport details and create a Pix key.

Tourist-first apps
💳

International Cards (Fallback)

Visa and Mastercard work at 95%+ of restaurants, hotels, and larger establishments. Note: a ~4.38% IOF tax applies to international card purchases in Brazil. For small vendors, street food, and markets — Pix or cash is often the only option.

Fallback only

✅

Action Before You Fly: Set Up Wise

Download Wise → create an account → add BRL as a currency → fund it from your home account. You'll arrive in Rio ready to pay anything with a QR code scan. No cash needed, no IOF surcharges, no bad exchange rates. This single step will make your week significantly smoother.

Local Cuisine

DISHES & DRINKS YOU MUST TRY

Brazilian food is deeply regional, joyful, and often misunderstood abroad. Rio has its own culinary identity — here's what to order, what to drink, and where to experience it properly.

🍖

Feijoada

Brazil's national dish. A slow-cooked black bean stew with pork (ribs, sausage, ear, feet — the whole animal). Served with white rice, farofa (toasted cassava flour), collard greens, and orange slices. Traditionally eaten on Saturdays. Heavy, rich, and absolutely worth it. Order it at Belmonte or Casa da Feijoada in Ipanema.

National dish
🥤

Caipirinha

Brazil's national cocktail. Cachaça (sugarcane spirit), lime, sugar, and ice. Simple, potent, perfect. The classic version is lime, but passion fruit (maracujá), strawberry, and kiwi variations are everywhere. If you only drink one thing in Brazil, this is it. Never order it with vodka — that's a Caipiroska, a different drink entirely.

National cocktail
🥐

Pão de Queijo

Warm, chewy cheese bread made from cassava flour and queijo minas. Eaten at breakfast, as a snack, or at any time of day. Addictive. Available at every padaria (bakery), café, and airport in Brazil. The ones from Minas Gerais state are considered the best.

Breakfast staple
🌴

Açaí

Not the smoothie bowl you know from LA. In Rio, açaí is thick, frozen, and served as a savory or sweet bowl with granola, banana, and guaraná syrup. On the beach at Ipanema and Copacabana, açaí vendors are everywhere. A post-surf staple and the perfect afternoon snack.

Beach essential
🫔

Coxinha & Salgados

Coxinha is a tear-drop shaped fried dough stuffed with shredded chicken — the ultimate Brazilian street snack. Found at every padaria and lanchonete. Other salgados (savory snacks) to try: esfiha, quibe, and pastel de queijo. Perfect for a quick lunch between meetings.

Street food
🍺

Chopp & Cerveja

Chopp is draft beer — blonde, ice cold, served in frozen mugs. The go-to drink at any boteco. Brazilian beer culture is serious: Brahma, Antarctica, and Itaipava dominate, but craft beer (cerveja artesanal) is booming. At a boteco, never let your glass warm up — the waiter will replace it.

Boteco culture
🦐

Moqueca Carioca

A Brazilian seafood stew with fish or shrimp, coconut milk, dendê (palm oil), tomato, and peppers. The Rio version (moqueca capixaba) is lighter than the Bahian version. Served in a clay pot at the table, still bubbling. Try it at Rubaiyat or Satyricon in Ipanema.

Must order
☕

Cafezinho

The Brazilian espresso — small, strong, sweet, served everywhere, always free after a meal or meeting. Refusing a cafezinho is almost an insult. It's a cultural ritual of hospitality. Accepting one is an easy way to signal respect and warmth to Brazilian counterparts.

Social ritual
🥥

Água de Coco

Fresh coconut water, served straight from the coconut on every beach in Rio. Naturally hydrating, sweet, and the best thing to drink in the heat. R–10 on the beach. Never drink from a bottle if you can have it fresh — there's no comparison.

Beach essential

🍽️

The Boteco is Rio's Living Room

The boteco (neighborhood bar) is where Rio truly lives. Plastic tables on the sidewalk, cold chopp, fried salgados, football on TV, loud conversation. Places like Bracarense (Leblon), Belmonte (Flamengo), and Bar Urca (Santa Teresa) are institutions. Going to a boteco with a Brazilian is an act of trust — it's where real conversations happen. Bring your business cards.
